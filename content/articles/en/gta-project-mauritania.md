In the shallow waters straddling the Mauritania–Senegal maritime border, one of sub-Saharan Africa's largest natural gas discoveries is finally reaching production. The Greater Tortue Ahmeyim (GTA) liquefied natural gas project — a joint venture between BP, Kosmos Energy, and the national oil companies of both nations — achieved first gas in early 2024 after years of delays and cost overruns.

The project's arrival is momentous. For Mauritania, a country of roughly 4.5 million people with a GDP hovering around $10 billion, GTA represents a structural break in its economic history. For the broader region, it signals the emergence of West Africa as a credible LNG supplier in a world rethinking its energy supply chains.

But the history of resource-rich developing nations counsels caution. Whether GTA transforms Mauritania or merely enriches a narrow class of elites depends entirely on choices that have nothing to do with geology.

## The Project at a Glance

Greater Tortue Ahmeyim sits in ultra-deep water approximately 100 kilometers offshore. Phase 1, the only phase currently sanctioned, will produce approximately **2.4 million tonnes per year (Mtpa)** of LNG from a floating liquefied natural gas vessel (FLNG) — the *Gimi*, a converted tanker operated by Golar LNG.

| Metric | Detail |
|---|---|
| Estimated reserves | ~15 trillion cubic feet |
| Phase 1 LNG capacity | 2.4 Mtpa |
| First gas | Q1 2024 |
| FLNG vessel | *Gimi* (Golar LNG) |
| Project partners | BP (operator), Kosmos Energy, PETROSEN, SMHPM |
| Phase 1 capex | ~$4.8 billion |
| Peak project capex (all phases) | ~$30 billion |

Revenues are split between the two host countries based on the maritime boundary, with Mauritania holding approximately 60% of the field by volume and Senegal 40%.

## What This Means for Mauritania's Economy

Mauritania's public finances have long depended on iron ore exports (through SNIM), fisheries royalties, and modest gold production. Gas changes this calculus dramatically — but on a delayed timeline.

First, royalty and profit-oil revenues will ramp slowly. Phase 1 at 2.4 Mtpa is relatively modest; meaningful fiscal flows require Phase 2 expansion (targeted at 10 Mtpa) which has yet to be formally sanctioned. BP and Kosmos face pressure from investors on capital discipline, and the global LNG market — which entered a structural surplus in 2026 — complicates the economics of further expansion.

> "GTA Phase 1 is a proof of concept, not a windfall. The windfall, if it comes, is Phase 2. And Phase 2 is not guaranteed." — Sahel energy analyst, 2024

Second, local content requirements remain underdeveloped. The upstream sector is entirely foreign-operated, with limited capacity in Mauritanian firms to supply services, logistics, or skilled labour at scale. SMHPM, the state oil company, has moved to establish partnerships, but institutional capacity constraints are real.

Third — and most critically — the Mauritanian government has yet to establish a credible sovereign wealth framework for managing gas revenues. The IMF and World Bank have recommended a dedicated Natural Resource Fund, ring-fenced from the general budget, but legislation remains stalled.

## The Regional Energy Geometry

GTA's arrival reshapes the regional energy map in important ways.

**Senegal's parallel acceleration.** Senegal's own offshore gas fields (SNE/Sangomar oilfield came online in 2024; additional gas blocks remain under exploration) position the country as a complementary energy exporter. Dakar has moved more aggressively than Nouakchott on downstream infrastructure, including a 400-km domestic gas pipeline to Tambacounda.

**Europe's diversification imperative.** Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine triggered a structural shift in European LNG demand. West African suppliers — Nigeria, Angola, Mozambique, and now Mauritania–Senegal — are primary beneficiaries. BP has pre-contracted the majority of GTA Phase 1 LNG for European delivery, insulating early revenues from spot-market volatility.

**The transition paradox.** Both Mauritania and Senegal have committed to net-zero pathways under the Paris Agreement. Gas, however, is framed domestically as a "transition fuel" — both nations argue that developing their gas reserves is compatible with long-term decarbonization, particularly given their low per-capita emissions. This framing has supporters (IEA's "developing nations' exception") and critics (those who argue any new fossil infrastructure locks in emissions for decades).

## The Risks Nobody Wants to Talk About

### Stranded Asset Risk

GTA's Phase 2 economics depend on a long-run LNG price that justifies $25+ billion in additional capital. As the global energy transition accelerates — particularly in Europe, GTA's primary market — demand trajectories beyond 2035 are deeply uncertain. Phase 2 FID (Final Investment Decision) may never come.

### Governance and Revenue Management

Mauritania's Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index score has improved marginally over the past decade but remains low (rank 147 of 180 in 2023). Without robust parliamentary oversight and an independent sovereign fund, gas revenues risk becoming a tool of political patronage rather than national development.

### Dutch Disease

A sudden influx of hard currency — even at Phase 1 levels — will appreciate the ouguiya and compress competitiveness in Mauritania's non-resource tradeable sectors: fisheries processing, light manufacturing, and agriculture. Managing this "Dutch Disease" effect requires active macroeconomic policy that Mauritania's central bank has limited capacity to implement.

## What Comes Next

The next 24 months are pivotal. BP must decide whether to sanction Phase 2 amid uncertain market conditions. Mauritania must pass its sovereign fund legislation before Phase 1 revenues begin flowing at scale. And SMHPM must begin building genuine institutional capacity — not just equity participation — in the upstream sector.

GTA is not a lottery ticket. It is a 30-year infrastructure project whose returns depend on geology, global energy markets, and — most critically — the quality of Mauritanian institutions.

The gas is there. The question is whether the country is ready.
