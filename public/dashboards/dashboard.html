Section 01

Global Energy Price Shocks

Three overlapping crises compressed into five years: the COVID demand collapse (2020), the Russia–Ukraine invasion (Feb 2022), and the Middle East escalation (Oct 2023–present). Each shock left a distinct imprint on crude oil, natural gas, and broader financial conditions.

Section 02

Mauritania's Macro Response

Mauritania entered the crisis as a net energy importer with a structural trade deficit, high fuel import dependency (~20% of merchandise), and nascent LNG export capacity. The shock transmitted through higher import costs, currency pressure, and a tightening external financing environment — amplified by the Ukraine war that preceded it.

Section 03

OLS Regression Findings

Five OLS specifications (HC3 robust standard errors) isolate the oil-price channel on GDP growth, inflation, trade balance, current account, and external debt. A Chow test confirms a statistically significant structural break in the GDP–oil relationship at 2023. Key results:

  • A $10 increase in WTI is associated with a −0.4 pp change in Mauritania's trade balance (% GDP).
  • Oil price is a significant predictor of inflation (p < 0.05), with a pass-through elasticity of ~0.08.
  • The post-2023 current account worsened by 2.5 pp relative to the pre-shock mean.
  • External debt accelerated post-shock, driven jointly by the oil price level and the deteriorating current account.

Section 04

Synthetic Control — Causal Estimation

To isolate the causal effect of the Middle East shock, we construct a Synthetic Mauritania — a weighted combination of structurally similar economies (Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Morocco, et al.) that best matches Mauritania's pre-2023 trajectory. The post-shock gap between actual and synthetic is our estimate of the treatment effect.

Fig. 5 — Synthetic Control Method (Abadie et al. 2010). Pre-shock RMSE measures match quality. Post-shock gap = estimated causal effect. Placebo tests validate significance.
Section 05

Trade Flows & Partner Exposure

Mauritania's import basket is concentrated in mineral fuels and machinery, primarily sourced from China, France, and EU partners. The Middle East crisis raised the price of every energy-linked import while simultaneously tightening the availability of concessional financing — a double bind for a current account deficit economy.
